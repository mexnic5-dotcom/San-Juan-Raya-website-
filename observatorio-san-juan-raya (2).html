// 002 — The Place

What is
San Juan Raya?

San Juan Raya is a small highland community in the municipality of Zapotitlán Salinas, Puebla — and one of México’s most extraordinary fossil sites. Beneath its arid hills lies an ancient Cretaceous seabed: ammonites, gastropods, bivalves and fossilised dinosaur footprints, some preserved for roughly 100 million years, from a time when this desert was the floor of a shallow tropical ocean.

Today those fossils draw visitors and researchers from around the world. Community guides lead trails through open-air paleontological sites and a local fossil museum, making San Juan Raya a globally recognised destination for paleotourism — set in the heart of one of the planet’s richest cactus landscapes.

The community sits inside the Tehuacán-Cuicatlán Biosphere Reserve — inscribed by UNESCO as a Mixed World Heritage Site for both its outstanding biodiversity and its living cultural landscape. It is the same protected darkness above this reserve that the observatory now opens to the sky.

// 003 — Mission Statement

What We Are

Science belongs to everyone.

Observatorio San Juan Raya is a community-built, community-operated astronomical observatory — a new, sustainable branch grafted onto the ecotourism model the community already runs for its fossils. The same ground that preserves a 100-million-year-old seabed now opens its extraordinarily dark nights to the universe.

It began with a single measurement: the nights here were exceptionally dark. Light-pollution data confirmed a rare natural resource hiding in plain sight, and astronomy became a way to create local livelihoods while protecting the night sky. Today it is San Juan Raya’s own trained guides who operate the telescopes and run the sessions.

“To look up at the sky and understand what you see — that is the beginning of every great civilization.”

— Community Vision, San Juan Raya
// 004 — Instrumentation

The Heart of
Our Observatory

// Sliding-roof observatory — the roof retracts fully to expose the instruments to the entire sky.

At the core of the site is a sliding-roof observatory: a motorised structure whose roof retracts completely, giving the instruments an unobstructed dome of sky. At its centre is a professional, motorised Celestron telescope for deep-sky exploration, backed by an array of more than fifty additional telescopes for large groups.

Darkness here is not assumed — it is measured and defended. Continuous sky-quality monitoring keeps the site beyond DarkSky International’s strict reference threshold, and 50+ locally trained guides run sessions every single night.

// 005 — Project History

From Vision
to Reality

PHASE ONE
April 2025
The Proposal
Astronomy meets the community
Nicolás Calderón — founder and lifelong astronomy enthusiast — approaches the San Juan Raya community with a proposal and his own light-pollution measurements, making the case to add astronomy as a new branch of the tour-based ecosystem the community already runs.
PHASE TWO
Apr – Aug 2025 · 4 months
Training the Guides
Building local expertise
Over four months, every existing tour guide is trained from the ground up. Nicolás delivers astronomy lessons rooted in real science and invites renowned astrophotographers and professional astronomers to work directly with the guides, sharpening the community’s expertise.
PHASE THREE
Aug – Oct 2025
Momentum & First Light
Funding the observatory
Astronomy is woven into the community’s existing experiences and quickly begins raising significant funds — including from purpose-driven astronomy groups. Nicolás attends events, venues and workshops across Mexico City, building a network of professors and leading outreach with alumni from prestigious universities to show them the value of San Juan Raya. With the funds raised, the community votes to reinvest in its astronomy branch: a sliding-roof observatory is built and paired with a professional, scientific-calibre motorised Celestron telescope for deep-sky exploration. Visitors multiply — from national astronomy champions to renowned astronomers and astrophotographers.
PHASE FOUR
Late 2025 – 2026
DarkSky & Noche de las Estrellas
Alliances and a national stage
An alliance is formed with Joshua Iván Muñoz Salazar, México’s DarkSky delegate. Together with the community they begin rigorous 24/7 light-level monitoring and commit to earning DarkSky certification — a process still under way. Nicolás and Joshua then build a scientific-grade proposal to compete against leading Ibero-American institutions and government representatives to host the prestigious Noche de las Estrellas — and win. San Juan Raya will host the event in November 2026.
PHASE FIVE
2026 · Ongoing
Daily Operations
A full-time operation
Nicolás now works full-time, coordinating each day which guides accompany which visitors and maintaining the activity schedule from Mexico City. Benito is tasked with downloading the light-level measurement data and delivering it to Nicolás, keeping the dark-sky record continuous.
NOW
2026
Growth
The site grows — and so do we
As the site grows, the community grows with it. Nicolás has raised venture-capital funding and loans to sustain that growth, and construction has now begun on a purpose-built astronomy auditorium designed to serve as both museum and planetarium.
// 006 — Conservation

Protecting the
night itself.

A dark sky is a resource that can be lost in a single generation of careless lighting. We measure it, monitor it, and advocate for responsible illumination so the community’s newest asset survives — for visitors, for science, and for the people who live beneath it.

// 007 — Site & Coordinates

Finding
San Juan Raya

i

Where we are

San Juan Raya, municipality of Zapotitlán Salinas, Puebla — inside the Tehuacán-Cuicatlán Biosphere Reserve, a UNESCO Mixed World Heritage site.

ii

Getting here

About 2.5–3 hours from the city of Puebla and ~40 minutes from Tehuacán, via Zapotitlán Salinas. The final kilometers are dirt road, passable with care.

iii

Best nights

Clear skies near new moon give optimal conditions. We recommend booking ahead and checking the public-session calendar.

iv

Stay & eat

The community ecotourism center offers lodging and local meals — so a day among fossils and a night among stars fit into one stay.
